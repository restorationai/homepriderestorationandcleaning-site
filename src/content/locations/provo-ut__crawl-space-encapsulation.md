## Why Provo Properties Are Especially Vulnerable to Crawl Space Moisture

Provo's climate swings hard. Winters regularly drop below 20°F, summers push past 95°F, and the relative humidity in the valley spikes every spring as snowpack melts off the Wasatch Range. That thermal cycling creates a vapor-drive problem: warm, moist air from the ground pushes upward into a cold crawl space, condenses on wood framing, and creates the exact conditions wood-decay fungi need to colonize.

Homes built before 1980 — and there are a lot of them in central Provo's older residential corridors near University Avenue and the blocks surrounding BYU campus — were typically constructed with vented crawl spaces following the building codes of that era. The logic was that outside air would dry things out. In a high-humidity valley environment, the opposite happens: vents bring in more moisture than they exhaust. Encapsulation seals the crawl space from ground vapor and outside air entirely, turning it into a semi-conditioned buffer zone that protects your framing, insulation, and subfloor year-round.

The expansive clay soils common in Provo's 84601 and 84604 ZIP codes compound the issue. Clay retains moisture long after rain or snowmelt, and that moisture migrates upward through the soil and through any gaps in an unprotected crawl space floor. A heavy-duty vapor barrier — typically 20-mil reinforced polyethylene — bonded to the ground and sealed to the foundation walls stops that migration at the source.

## Our Crawl Space Encapsulation Process in Provo

Every encapsulation project starts with a full crawl space inspection: we measure moisture levels in the wood framing with a calibrated pin meter, check for existing mold or pest activity, assess the condition of any existing vapor barrier, and document the foundation wall type. Provo has a mix of poured concrete, concrete block, and older brick foundations — each requires a slightly different approach for sealing the wall-to-floor seam.

Once the crawl space is cleared and prepped, the process moves through these stages:

1. **Debris removal and minor remediation** — old fiberglass batt insulation that's fallen or become moisture-saturated is pulled and disposed of; any mold growth on joists is treated per IICRC S520 protocol before encapsulation begins.
2. **Ground vapor barrier installation** — 20-mil reinforced poly is laid across the entire crawl space floor, overlapped at seams by at least 12 inches, and sealed with butyl tape.
3. **Wall and pier wrapping** — the barrier is run up foundation walls and attached with termination bar and masonry fasteners, then sealed with closed-cell spray foam at the top edge. Piers are wrapped individually.
4. **Vent sealing** — existing foundation vents are plugged with rigid foam and sealed, converting the space from vented to unvented.
5. **Drainage and humidity control** — depending on moisture readings, we may install a crawl space dehumidifier sized for your square footage, with a condensate line routed to daylight or a sump.
