## Why Pleasant Grove Crawl Spaces Are Under Pressure

The geology here matters more than most homeowners realize. The Provo Bay shoreline of ancient Lake Bonneville left behind silty, expansive soils across much of northern Utah County, and Pleasant Grove is squarely in that zone. When those soils absorb snowmelt or irrigation water, they swell against foundation walls and push moisture vapor upward through unprotected crawl space dirt floors. Homes built in the 1960s and 1970s — a significant portion of the housing stock in established Pleasant Grove neighborhoods — typically have vented crawl spaces designed to "breathe," a standard that building science has largely reversed. Vented crawl spaces in this climate import humid outside air in summer and cold, condensation-prone air in winter, making the original design work against you.

Utah Lake's proximity adds a secondary humidity load that inland Wasatch Front cities like Lehi or American Fork experience less acutely. On still summer evenings, relative humidity near the lake shore can run 15–20 points higher than in the foothills, and that gradient pulls moisture toward any unprotected crawl space opening.

## Our Crawl Space Encapsulation Process in Pleasant Grove

Every encapsulation project starts with a full crawl space inspection — not a sales walkthrough, but a documented assessment of standing moisture, existing vapor barrier condition, insulation state, wood moisture content, and any signs of pest activity or prior water intrusion. In Pleasant Grove's older ranch-style homes, we frequently find fiberglass batt insulation stapled between joists that has absorbed enough moisture to sag and lose R-value entirely; that gets removed before any liner goes down.

The encapsulation itself uses a reinforced polyethylene liner — minimum 20-mil on the floor, sealed and mechanically fastened to the foundation walls and piers. Seams overlap by at least 12 inches and are taped with a manufacturer-approved seam tape rated for the temperature swings Utah County sees between January and July. Foundation vents are sealed with rigid foam inserts and foam-sealed at the perimeter. Where the inspection reveals active moisture intrusion, we address drainage or grading issues first; encapsulation without resolving the water source is a short-term fix.

Depending on the square footage and existing ventilation, we'll recommend either a passive or active dehumidification solution. Crawl spaces under 1,000 square feet with good air sealing often perform well with passive conditioning tied to the home's HVAC; larger or wetter spaces benefit from a dedicated crawl space dehumidifier with a condensate drain line routed to daylight.
