## Why Provo Properties See Water Damage Issues

Provo's geography creates a specific set of risk factors that don't show up the same way in warmer, lower-elevation Utah cities. The valley floor is underlain by clay-heavy soils left behind by ancient Lake Bonneville — the same prehistoric lake that formed the Bonneville Salt Flats west of the city. Clay soil absorbs water slowly and holds it longer, which means heavy rain or rapid snowmelt doesn't drain away; it pools against foundation walls and finds any crack or cold joint it can. Homes in the 84601 ZIP code, particularly older properties closer to downtown, were often built before modern waterproofing standards and can be especially vulnerable to lateral groundwater intrusion.

Beyond soil and geology, Provo's housing stock spans a wide age range. Neighborhoods near the Joaquin and Carterville areas include homes built in the 1940s through 1960s with galvanized steel supply lines that have long since reached the end of their service life. Galvanized pipe corrodes from the inside out, restricting flow until a section finally gives way — often inside a wall cavity where no one notices until water is wicking through baseboards.

## Our Water Damage Restoration Process in Provo

Every job starts with a thorough moisture assessment using thermal imaging cameras and calibrated moisture meters. Water hides — behind tile, inside wall cavities, under engineered hardwood — and what you can see on the surface is rarely the full picture. Once we've mapped the affected area, we deploy truck-mounted and portable extraction units to pull standing water before it migrates further into subfloor assemblies.

Structural drying in Provo's climate requires careful calibration. At higher elevation with lower average humidity, the air can actually assist drying — but only when equipment is set correctly for the ambient conditions. Our IICRC-certified technicians calculate the number and placement of commercial-grade desiccant dehumidifiers and high-velocity air movers based on the specific psychrometric readings in your home, not a one-size-fits-all formula. Moisture readings are logged daily so you have a documented drying record — something your insurance adjuster will ask for.

Anticrobial treatment of affected surfaces follows extraction, and any building materials that can't be dried to IICRC S500 standard — saturated insulation, compromised drywall — are removed and documented for your claim before reconstruction begins.
