Park City's snowmelt season hits fast. If your basement is taking on water right now, you're likely dealing with that exact pressure: hydrostatic force pushing through foundation walls or floor seams, not a simple pipe drip. ## Why Park City Basements Flood Differently Than Most Utah Homes

At 6,900 feet elevation, Park City sits in a climate band where freeze-thaw cycles happen dozens of times each winter — not just in January, but well into spring. That repeated expansion and contraction works on foundation mortar, concrete block joints, and window well seals in ways that lower-elevation homes in the Salt Lake Valley rarely experience. By the time a homeowner notices water on the basement floor, the entry point has often been widening for years.

The housing stock adds another layer of complexity. Many homes in the Old Town area were built in the late 1800s and early 1900s to house silver miners, with stone or unreinforced block foundations that were never designed to manage modern snowmelt volumes. Newer construction in the Deer Valley and Snyderville Basin areas tends to have better waterproofing, but those homes often sit on steeper lots where surface water channels directly toward the structure during heavy melt events. Neither profile is simple to dry out.

Soil composition around Park City also matters. The mix of clay-heavy soils and decomposed granite common on the mountain's flanks drains poorly when saturated, meaning water lingers against foundation walls far longer than it would in sandy valley soils. That extended contact time is exactly the window in which mold colonization begins — typically within 24 to 48 hours of initial saturation.

## Our Basement Flooding Cleanup Process in Park City

When we arrive, the first priority is stopping the water source if it's still active — whether that's a sump pump that failed during a power outage, a backed-up floor drain, or active seepage through a foundation wall. We don't start drying until the inflow is controlled.

From there, the process moves through several concrete steps:

- **Extraction** — Truck-mounted and portable extractors pull standing water from the floor. In finished basements, we check under floating floors and behind baseboards, where water migrates invisibly.
- **Moisture mapping** — Thermal imaging and calibrated moisture meters document the full extent of saturation, including walls and subfloor assemblies. This documentation also matters for your insurance claim.
- **Structural drying** — Industrial desiccant dehumidifiers and high-velocity air movers run in a calculated configuration based on the room's cubic footage and the measured grain depression. At Park City's altitude, ambient air holds less moisture than at sea level, which actually aids evaporation — but the colder baseline temperatures mean heating the space is often necessary to keep drying rates on track.
- **Antimicrobial treatment** — Applied to all affected surfaces before any reconstruction begins.
- **Final clearance readings** — We don't pack up equipment until moisture readings return to pre-loss baselines, documented in writing.

All work is performed by IICRC Certified technicians under license #RC-25-0737.

One thing that surprises homeowners in Old Town Park City: the original stone foundations on many of those century-old homes don't just let water in through cracks — the stone itself is porous and wicks moisture upward into the framing above the foundation line.
