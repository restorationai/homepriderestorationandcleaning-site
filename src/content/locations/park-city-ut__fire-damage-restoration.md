At 7,000 feet, Park City's dry mountain air and low humidity might seem like fire's worst enemy — and they are. The same conditions that make the Wasatch Back a world-class ski destination also mean fires spread faster, smoke penetrates deeper into porous materials, and the acrid residue left behind by burning pine and cedar (common in older Park City construction) bonds to surfaces more aggressively than in valley homes. When fire damage hits a property here, the clock starts running the moment the last flame is out.

## Why Park City Properties Face Distinct Fire Damage Challenges

Park City's housing stock is unusually varied for a Utah mountain town. You'll find everything from 1970s-era ski chalets with exposed timber framing and tongue-and-groove pine ceilings to newer high-end developments with smart-home wiring and engineered materials. Both present specific problems after a fire. Older wood-heavy interiors absorb smoke oils deeply into the grain — surface cleaning alone won't eliminate odor or prevent long-term staining. Newer homes with spray-foam insulation and composite materials can off-gas toxic byproducts long after the fire is extinguished, complicating air quality recovery.

The altitude matters in ways that aren't obvious. At elevation, combustion is slightly less complete, which means fires in Park City often produce more particulate-laden, oily smoke than the same fire at valley elevation. That smoke travels into HVAC systems, gets trapped behind knee walls in chalet-style rooflines, and settles into the fibers of high-end furnishings that are common in this market. Restoration here isn't just cleanup — it's a systematic decontamination process.

## Our Fire Damage Restoration Process in Park City

Every fire job starts with a full structural and contents assessment before a single cleaning tool comes out. We document damage with photo and moisture-mapping records — essential for insurance claims and for tracking hidden smoke migration through wall cavities.

From there, the process moves in a defined sequence:

- **Emergency board-up and tarping** to secure the structure against weather and unauthorized entry. Park City winters are unforgiving; an unsecured roof opening after a fire can compound damage within hours.
- **Smoke and soot removal** using dry-chemical sponges on porous surfaces, alkaline cleaners on protein-based residues, and HEPA-filtered negative air machines to capture particulates without redistributing them.
- **Odor neutralization** with thermal fogging and hydroxyl generation — methods that reach into wall cavities and subfloor spaces where smoke has migrated, not just the surfaces you can see.
- **Structural drying and dehumidification** where firefighting water has been introduced. At Park City's elevation, evaporation rates behave differently than at lower altitudes, and we calibrate our drying equipment settings accordingly.
- **Reconstruction coordination**, from framing and drywall to finish carpentry, handled under our general contractor license (#RC-25-0737) so you're dealing with one company through the entire process.

Our IICRC-certified technicians follow S700 fire and smoke restoration standards on every job, which matters when your adjuster is reviewing the scope of work.
