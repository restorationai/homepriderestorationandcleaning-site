## Why Lehi Properties Face Elevated Crawl Space Moisture Problems

Lehi sits at roughly 4,550 feet elevation, which means it gets the full swing of Utah's continental climate — hot, dry summers followed by winters that push hard freezes down through the soil. That freeze-thaw cycle is relentless on crawl space foundations. Cracks that are hairline in October can be finger-width by March.

Beyond the soil and climate, the sheer pace of construction in Lehi over the last decade has produced a large number of homes built quickly on lots that weren't always graded with long-term drainage in mind. Neighborhoods near the older core of the city — closer to Main Street and the historic downtown — tend to have homes from the 1970s and 1980s with original fiberglass batt insulation stapled between joists. That insulation traps moisture against the wood rather than managing it, and once it gets wet it stays wet. Newer construction in the 84043 and 84045 ZIP codes often has no vapor barrier at all, or a thin 6-mil poly sheet that has torn or shifted at the seams.

Jordan River tributaries and irrigation canals that run through western Lehi also raise the water table in certain areas seasonally, which can push ground moisture up through bare dirt crawl spaces faster than most homeowners expect.

## Our Crawl Space Encapsulation Process in Lehi

Encapsulation is not a single product — it's a system, and every component matters. Here's how we approach it on a Lehi home:

**Assessment first.** Before any material goes in, a technician inspects the crawl space for standing water, existing vapor barrier condition, wood rot, insulation damage, and any signs of pest intrusion. We photograph everything.

**Drainage and moisture control.** If ground water is actively entering the space, we address that before sealing. A vapor barrier laid over a wet crawl space traps the problem rather than solving it.

**20-mil reinforced liner installation.** We use a heavy-duty, puncture-resistant polyethylene liner — not the 6-mil sheeting sold at hardware stores. The liner runs up the stem walls and is mechanically fastened and taped at every seam. On Lehi homes with irregular footings or concrete piers, we cut and lap the liner carefully so there are no gaps where the ground is still exposed.

**Insulation and conditioning.** Depending on the home's HVAC setup and the crawl space dimensions, we may recommend closed-cell spray foam on the rim joists or a conditioned crawl space approach that ties into the home's air system. We'll explain the tradeoffs honestly — a conditioned crawl space costs more upfront but performs better in Utah's climate extremes.

**Final inspection and documentation.** We walk you through the finished space, explain the warranty on materials and labor, and provide documentation you can keep for insurance or resale purposes.

## Local Note: What Lehi's Clay Soil Means for Liner Longevity

One thing we've learned working on homes near the older Lehi townsite and out toward the newer developments along SR-73: when bentonite clay is present under a crawl space, it doesn't just hold moisture — it moves. That movement can slowly push a liner that's only taped at the seams into wrinkles and gaps over a few seasons. We use mechanical fasteners along the stem wall termination bar in addition to tape, and we overlap seams by a minimum of 12 inches rather than the 6-inch standard. It adds time to the installation, but on Utah County clay it's the difference between a liner that lasts 15 years and one that needs re-work in five.
