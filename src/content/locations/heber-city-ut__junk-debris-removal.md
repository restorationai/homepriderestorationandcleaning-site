## Why Heber City Properties Accumulate Junk Differently

The valley's elevation and its mix of year-round residences and vacation properties creates a debris profile you don't see in lower-elevation Utah communities. Seasonal homeowners often leave behind appliances, mattresses, and construction scraps when they sell or renovate, because hauling anything over Parley's Canyon or through Provo Canyon feels like a project in itself. Year-round residents, meanwhile, tend to defer garage and shed cleanouts until spring — and by then, a winter's worth of broken equipment, holiday packaging, and storm debris has piled up alongside whatever was already there.

Older homes in the valley, many built in the 1960s and 1970s when the area was primarily agricultural, frequently have outbuildings — chicken coops, hay sheds, detached workshops — that have been repurposed as storage for decades. Clearing these structures means dealing with materials that predate modern disposal categories: old treated lumber, asbestos-containing floor tiles, lead-based paint on metal shelving. We sort carefully and route materials to the appropriate disposal facilities, including Wasatch County's transfer station on 600 West, rather than lumping everything into a single roll-off.
