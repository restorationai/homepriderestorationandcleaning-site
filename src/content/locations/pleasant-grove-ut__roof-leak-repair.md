## Why Pleasant Grove Properties See Roof Leak Damage

The Wasatch Front's freeze-thaw cycle is one of the most punishing conditions a residential roof faces. Pleasant Grove sits at roughly 4,800 feet elevation, which means winter temperatures regularly swing from single digits overnight to the mid-40s by afternoon. That daily cycling forces ice and meltwater under shingles repeatedly throughout January and February, widening micro-cracks in flashing and sealant that might hold fine in a milder climate. By the time April arrives, those gaps are ready to let a serious rainstorm straight through.

The housing stock adds another layer of complexity. A significant portion of Pleasant Grove's established neighborhoods — particularly the older subdivisions closer to State Street and Center Street — feature homes built in the 1970s and 1980s with low-slope roof sections over garage additions and covered patios. Those shallow-pitch areas drain slowly and are especially vulnerable to ponding during the heavy convective storms that roll in off Utah Lake on summer afternoons. Attic insulation in homes of that era is often fiberglass batt rather than blown-in, which means saturated insulation loses its R-value fast and holds moisture against the roof sheathing for days.

From there, the process moves through four stages:

3. **Extraction and drying** — commercial desiccant dehumidifiers and air movers positioned to pull moisture out of structural cavities, not just surface materials.
4. **Restoration** — replacing damaged drywall, insulation, and interior finishes, and documenting everything for your insurance claim.
