## Why Park City Properties Are Prone to Sewage Backups

The terrain and the building stock here create a specific set of vulnerabilities. Many homes in the Old Town area — particularly those built during Park City's mining-era revival in the 1970s and 1980s — have lateral sewer lines that run long distances downhill to connect with Summit County's municipal system. Those laterals shift over decades as the ground freezes and thaws, developing low spots, root intrusion points, and hairline cracks that let groundwater in and sewage out. When snowmelt saturates the soil in spring, municipal lines can surcharge, pushing raw sewage back up through floor drains and toilets with very little warning.

Homes in the Prospector area and along lower Kearns Boulevard face a slightly different pattern: higher-density development means shared utility corridors, and a blockage in one unit's lateral can affect neighbors before anyone realizes the source. Vacation rental properties compound the problem — a backup that sits undetected over a long weekend can allow sewage-borne pathogens to colonize porous materials within 24 to 48 hours.

## Our Sewage Cleanup and Sanitization Process in Park City

When we arrive on-site, the first priority is containment — stopping cross-contamination from spreading to unaffected rooms. We establish negative air pressure in the work zone using HEPA-filtered air scrubbers, then begin extraction of standing sewage using truck-mounted and portable units capable of handling solid waste. Every surface that has contacted Category 3 water — flooring, baseboards, lower wall cavities — is assessed for salvageability before anything is removed.

Sanitization follows extraction. We apply EPA-registered disinfectants rated for sewage contamination, not general-purpose cleaners. Porous materials that can't be fully decontaminated — certain types of subflooring, insulation, drywall below the flood line — are bagged, documented, and disposed of according to Summit County waste handling requirements. Because Park City's altitude affects drying rates in ways that differ from lower-elevation jobs, we adjust our drying equipment placement and monitoring intervals accordingly; what dries in 48 hours in Salt Lake City may take 72 or more hours at 7,000 feet due to the way moisture behaves in thinner air. Our technicians are IICRC Certified and follow the S500 standard for water damage and the S520 protocol where mold risk is elevated.

One thing we've learned working in Park City specifically: many of the older vacation condos in the 84060 ZIP code were built with cast-iron drain stacks that have never been replaced.
