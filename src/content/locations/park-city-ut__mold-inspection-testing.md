Park City's elevation sits above 6,900 feet, and the swing between a wet spring snowmelt and a dry summer can leave moisture trapped inside walls and crawl spaces long before a homeowner notices a musty smell. At that altitude, temperature differentials between a heated interior and a cold exterior wall are more extreme than anywhere in the Wasatch Front valleys — and that gap is exactly where condensation feeds mold colonies. If you've spotted discoloration near a window frame, caught an earthy odor in a basement, or recently dealt with a slow leak from snowpack pressing against a foundation, a professional mold inspection and testing assessment is the right first step.

## Why Park City Properties See Elevated Mold Risk

The same conditions that make Park City one of Utah's most desirable mountain communities also make its homes structurally vulnerable to hidden moisture. Heavy annual snowfall — often exceeding 300 inches at the upper resorts — creates prolonged roof loading and ice-dam cycles. When ice dams form along eaves, meltwater backs up under shingles and can seep into attic sheathing and insulation without ever producing a visible ceiling stain. By the time a homeowner notices anything, mold may have been colonizing wood framing for weeks.

The housing stock adds another layer of complexity. Many properties in the Old Town area were built in the late 1800s and early 1900s as silver-mining-era worker housing. Those older structures often have minimal vapor barriers, original cellulose insulation, and stone or brick foundations that wick ground moisture directly into interior wall cavities. Newer luxury developments — particularly the ski-in/ski-out properties in the Deer Valley corridor — tend to have sophisticated HVAC systems, but those systems can spread spores through ductwork if a mold source goes undetected. Neither building type is immune; they just fail in different ways.

## Our Mold Inspection and Testing Process in Park City

A thorough mold assessment here isn't the same as a quick visual walkthrough. The process starts with a full moisture mapping of the property using thermal imaging and calibrated moisture meters. In Park City homes, we pay particular attention to north-facing walls, crawl spaces under decks, and the underside of roof sheathing in attic spaces — all areas where cold-surface condensation accumulates through the ski season.

After the physical inspection, we collect air samples and, where surface growth is visible or suspected, swab samples for laboratory analysis. Air samples establish a baseline spore count for each room and compare it against an outdoor control sample — a critical step because outdoor mold spore levels in mountain environments differ significantly from those in the Salt Lake Valley. Swab samples identify the specific genera present, which matters when determining remediation scope and whether any species warrant additional precautions. Lab results typically return within 24–48 hours, and we walk you through the findings in plain language, not just a PDF with numbers.
