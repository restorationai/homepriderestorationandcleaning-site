## Why Provo Properties Take Storm Hits Differently

Utah Valley's semi-arid climate fools a lot of homeowners. Because it's dry most of the year, roofing materials, caulk, and window seals tend to dry out and micro-crack between seasons — leaving homes more vulnerable than they look when a genuine storm arrives. Hail is the biggest culprit. Provo and the surrounding valley average several significant hail events per year, and asphalt shingles that look fine from the ground can have hundreds of impact fractures that only show up under close inspection or, worse, during the next heavy rain.

The housing stock adds another layer of complexity. Neighborhoods near Brigham Young University include a mix of mid-century brick homes and older wood-frame construction. Brick facades handle wind reasonably well but can allow water intrusion at the mortar joints after hail impact, and that moisture travels quietly into wall cavities for days before anyone notices a stain on the drywall. Flat-roofed commercial properties and older apartment buildings in the downtown corridor face their own drainage challenges — ponding water after a storm can compromise a roof membrane within 24 to 48 hours if it isn't addressed.

The Jordan River corridor and low-lying streets near Utah Lake are also worth noting. When heavy rain follows a dry spell, compacted soil sheds water instead of absorbing it, and that runoff has to go somewhere — often into crawlspaces and finished basements.

## Our Storm Damage Restoration Process in Provo

Every job starts with a full exterior and interior assessment before a single tool comes off the truck. We document everything with photos and written notes structured to match what your insurance adjuster needs — this step alone prevents weeks of back-and-forth on claims.

From there, the process follows the damage:

**Tarping and board-up.** Compromised roofs and broken windows get secured immediately to stop secondary water intrusion. In Provo's afternoon wind patterns, an unsecured tarp is a liability, so we use mechanical fastening rather than weight-only systems.

**Water extraction and drying.** If the storm drove rain or hail melt into the structure, we deploy truck-mounted extractors and commercial-grade desiccant or refrigerant dehumidifiers calibrated for Utah's elevation (roughly 4,550 feet above sea level — equipment that's sized for sea-level conditions underperforms here).

**Debris removal and structural assessment.** Tree limbs, roofing material, and damaged siding are cleared and inventoried. A structural technician checks load-bearing elements, particularly in older wood-frame homes where a fallen tree can transfer stress in non-obvious ways.

**Reconstruction.** Once the structure is dry and stable, our licensed reconstruction team — operating under Utah contractor license RC-25-0737 — handles roofing, siding, windows, and interior finishes. One company, start to finish, means no handoff gaps.

Our IICRC-certified technicians follow S500 and S520 standards throughout, which matters when your adjuster is reviewing the scope of work.
