## Why Park City Properties See More Water Damage Than You'd Expect

The obvious culprit is snow — 300-plus inches of it in a good season. But the real damage often starts with snowmelt that has nowhere to go. Park City's terrain channels runoff toward foundations, and many homes in the historic Old Town area were built in the late 1800s and early 1900s, long before modern waterproofing standards. Stone and brick foundations in that neighborhood absorb groundwater readily, and basement intrusion during spring melt is nearly a rite of passage for Old Town homeowners.

Higher up the mountain, newer construction in communities like Promontory or Tuhaye faces a different problem: homes that sit vacant for weeks or months at a time. A slow leak behind a dishwasher or a failed supply line under a bathroom vanity can run undetected for days. By the time a property manager or owner arrives, you're not dealing with a wet floor — you're dealing with saturated subfloor, compromised cabinetry, and the early stages of mold colonization. Mold can begin colonizing wet organic material within 24 to 72 hours at typical indoor temperatures, which means a weekend away can turn a minor drip into a major remediation project.

## Our Water Damage Restoration Process in Park City

When we arrive on-site, the first priority is stopping active water intrusion and documenting the scope before anything is moved or disturbed — especially important for insurance claims. From there, the process follows a structured sequence:

**Water extraction** comes first. We use truck-mounted and portable extraction units to pull standing water from hardwood floors, carpet, tile, and subfloor assemblies. In Park City's older homes, where tongue-and-groove hardwood flooring is common, we work carefully to preserve the material where possible rather than defaulting to tear-out.

**Moisture mapping** follows immediately. Thermal imaging cameras and calibrated moisture meters let us trace water migration behind walls and under flooring without unnecessary demolition. Water travels farther than it looks — especially in homes with radiant heat systems, which are common in Park City and can wick moisture along tubing channels inside the slab or subfloor.

**Structural drying** is where the elevation matters. At 7,000 feet, ambient air holds less moisture than at sea level, which sounds helpful — and it can be — but it also means our drying calculations are calibrated differently than they would be in Salt Lake City or St. George. We adjust airflow and dehumidifier placement to account for the thinner air and monitor psychrometric readings daily until materials reach target moisture content.

**Final documentation** includes moisture logs, equipment placement records, and drying validation reports — the paperwork your insurance adjuster will ask for.

## Local Note: Radiant Heat Systems and Hidden Moisture Pathways

One thing that catches out-of-town contractors in Park City: the prevalence of in-floor radiant heat systems, particularly in higher-end homes and newer construction in neighborhoods like Silver Springs and the Canyons Village area. When a water loss occurs near a radiant system, moisture doesn't behave the way it does in a conventionally framed floor. It follows the tubing channels and can travel 10 to 15 feet from the visible wet zone before showing up on a moisture meter. We've seen cases where a refrigerator line failure in a kitchen soaked a living room subfloor on the other side of a wall — entirely because the water tracked along a radiant loop channel. If your restoration contractor doesn't ask about your heating system before they start drying, that's a problem.
