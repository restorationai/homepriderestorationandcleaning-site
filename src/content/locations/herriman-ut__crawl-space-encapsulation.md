Herriman sits at the foot of the Oquirrh Mountains on a bench of expansive clay soil that swells when it gets wet and shrinks when it dries — a cycle that repeats every spring snowmelt and every summer monsoon surge. That ground movement works directly against crawl space vents, vapor barriers, and rim joists, making moisture intrusion one of the most persistent structural threats in homes throughout the 84096 ZIP code. If you've noticed a musty smell rising through your floors, wood framing that feels soft underfoot, or condensation collecting on your HVAC ducts, the crawl space is almost always where the problem starts.

## Why Herriman Properties Are Especially Vulnerable to Crawl Space Moisture

Homes built during Herriman's rapid growth years — roughly 2000 through 2015 — were constructed quickly on large subdivided lots across the city's western bench. Many of those builds used traditional vented crawl spaces, which made sense on paper but perform poorly against Utah's swing between dry winters and wet spring runoff. When snowpack in the Oquirrhs melts, water follows the natural grade toward the valley floor, and homes on the lower-lying streets near Herriman's newer developments can see standing water accumulate under the subfloor within 48 hours of a heavy melt event.

Compounding the issue is Herriman's clay-heavy soil. Unlike sandy or loam soils that drain quickly, clay holds moisture close to the surface for weeks. That prolonged contact with the foundation perimeter drives relative humidity inside unencapsulated crawl spaces above 70% — the threshold at which mold colonization typically begins within 24 to 48 hours on wood framing.

## Our Crawl Space Encapsulation Process in Herriman

Encapsulation in this climate isn't just rolling out a sheet of plastic and calling it done. The process we follow is calibrated to Herriman's specific conditions:

**1. Inspection and moisture mapping.** We measure current humidity levels, probe framing for soft spots, and check for any existing mold or pest activity before a single tool comes out of the truck. In homes near the Herriman Hills area, we pay particular attention to the uphill side of the crawl space where lateral water migration tends to enter first.

**2. Drainage and prep.** If standing water or saturated soil is present, we address it before encapsulating. Trapping moisture under a vapor barrier accelerates the damage it was meant to prevent.

**3. Heavy-duty vapor barrier installation.** We install a 20-mil reinforced polyethylene liner across the floor and up the foundation walls, sealed at all seams and penetrations with manufacturer-spec tape. In Herriman's climate, thinner barriers degrade faster due to temperature swings between summer highs above 95°F and subzero winter nights.

**4. Rim joist insulation and air sealing.** The rim joist is where conditioned interior air and exterior cold or heat meet — a major condensation point in Utah's climate. We seal and insulate each bay to stop that thermal bridge.

**5. Ventilation or dehumidification.** Depending on the crawl space's configuration, we either seal existing vents and install a dedicated crawl space dehumidifier or integrate the space with the home's conditioned air system. Both approaches are code-compliant under Utah's current residential building standards.
