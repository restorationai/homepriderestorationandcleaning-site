## Why Provo Homes Hold Odors Longer Than You'd Expect

The Wasatch Front's low humidity — often 15–25% relative humidity in summer — sounds like it should help odors dissipate. In practice, it does the opposite. Dry air allows volatile organic compounds (VOCs) from smoke, mold, or decomposition to stay airborne longer rather than binding to moisture particles and settling out. At the same time, Provo's older housing stock — particularly the brick bungalows and Craftsman-era homes clustered near the BYU campus and along Center Street — tends to have original plaster walls, single-pane windows, and minimal vapor barriers. Plaster is far more absorptive than modern drywall, pulling smoke and odor molecules into the substrate where surface sprays can't reach them. A house that flooded or burned in a neighborhood like Joaquin or Timp can hold odor memory in its walls for months if the source isn't treated at the molecular level.

## Our Odor Removal and Deodorization Process in Provo

Effective odor elimination is not a single step — it's a sequenced protocol calibrated to the source, the building materials, and the environment. Here's how a typical job runs:

### 1. Source Identification and Assessment
Before any equipment goes in, a technician walks the structure with a moisture meter, thermal camera, and odor mapping checklist. Odors from a water loss smell different from fire smoke, which smells different from biological sources — and each requires a different chemistry. We document the affected zones and note material types, because plaster, brick, and original hardwood common in Provo's pre-1960 homes absorb and release odor compounds on a different timeline than OSB or vinyl flooring.

### 2. Source Removal
No deodorization technology eliminates an odor that still has an active source. Charred framing, saturated insulation, or contaminated carpet padding gets removed before treatment begins. Skipping this step is the most common reason a "deodorized" home still smells six weeks later.

### 3. Thermal Fogging
For smoke and fire odor, thermal fogging is often the most effective primary treatment. A petroleum-based or water-based deodorizing agent is converted to a fine fog that penetrates the same pathways smoke traveled — wall cavities, HVAC ducts, attic spaces. The fog particles bond to odor molecules at the molecular level and neutralize them rather than masking them. We adjust fog density and dwell time based on Provo's lower air pressure at elevation, which affects aerosol particle behavior.

### 4. Ozone or Hydroxyl Treatment
After fogging, we deploy either ozone generators or hydroxyl generators depending on occupancy status and material sensitivity. Ozone is faster and more aggressive — effective for severe smoke odor in vacant structures — but requires full evacuation of people, pets, and plants. Hydroxyl generators are safe for occupied spaces and work well for ongoing biological odors (pet, mold, decomposition). Both technologies oxidize odor-causing molecules rather than covering them.

### 5. Encapsulation and Final Air Quality Check
For porous surfaces that can't be removed, an odor-blocking encapsulant seals residual compounds. We finish with an air quality walkthrough and, when requested, third-party air sampling to confirm clearance.

## Local Note: Altitude Changes How Ozone Equipment Is Calibrated

This is something that matters in Provo but rarely comes up in contractor conversations: ozone generators are rated at sea level. At Provo's elevation of approximately 4,549 feet, air is roughly 15% less dense, which means an ozone machine produces meaningfully less ozone per hour than its spec sheet suggests. Technicians who don't account for this run treatments that are too short and leave jobs that smell fine on day one but rebound within a week. Home Pride's crews calibrate dwell times and machine output for Utah County elevations — it's a small adjustment that makes a significant difference in whether a treatment holds.
