Homes built during the city's explosive expansion in the 2000s and 2010s were often set on shallow crawl space foundations over expansive clay soils — soils that heave with winter frost and then dry-shrink in the blazing summer heat, leaving gaps that pull outside air, moisture, and pests directly under your living space. If you've noticed musty odors rising through your floors, higher-than-normal heating bills, or frost on your rim joists in January, crawl space encapsulation is almost certainly the fix.

## Why Eagle Mountain Crawl Spaces Develop Problems Faster Than You'd Expect

The elevation and semi-arid climate here fool a lot of homeowners. Because annual precipitation is low — typically under 12 inches — it's easy to assume moisture isn't a threat. But Eagle Mountain's temperature swings are extreme: summer highs above 95°F and winter lows that regularly drop below 10°F. That thermal cycling drives condensation inside unencapsulated crawl spaces every single night during shoulder seasons, when warm daytime air enters the vents and hits cold concrete or soil. Within 24 to 48 hours of repeated condensation events, wood framing can reach moisture content levels where mold begins to colonize.

The clay-heavy soils common throughout the area compound this. After a wet spring — even a mild one — that clay holds capillary moisture for months and releases it slowly as vapor. Fiberglass batt insulation stapled between floor joists, still common in homes built here before 2015, absorbs that vapor and sags, losing R-value and creating a perfect habitat for mold and rodents. Encapsulation replaces that cycle with a sealed, controlled environment.

## Our Crawl Space Encapsulation Process in Eagle Mountain

Every encapsulation job starts with a thorough inspection — we document existing insulation condition, wood moisture readings, any standing water or efflorescence on the foundation walls, and the current ventilation setup. In Eagle Mountain, we frequently find builder-grade vented crawl spaces that were code-compliant at the time of construction but are now understood to be counterproductive in this climate zone.

From there, the process follows a specific sequence:

1. **Remove and dispose of old insulation** — saturated or moldy batts come out entirely before any barrier goes down.
2. **Address any active moisture intrusion** — if grading, downspout discharge, or foundation cracks are feeding water in, we document those findings and coordinate repairs before sealing.
3. **Install a 20-mil reinforced polyethylene vapor barrier** — we run it up the foundation walls and seal it to the rim joist with closed-cell spray foam, leaving no pathway for ground vapor.
4. **Seal all vents and penetrations** — in Eagle Mountain's climate, converting to a conditioned or semi-conditioned crawl space dramatically outperforms the old passive-vent model.
5. **Install a crawl space dehumidifier if needed** — homes with a history of high humidity readings or visible mold often benefit from a dedicated unit with a condensate pump.

We're IICRC Certified and licensed in Utah (License #RC-25-0737), so the work meets state contractor standards and can be documented for HOA or lender review.

## Local Note: What We See Specifically in Eagle Mountain's Newer Subdivisions

A pattern we've noticed repeatedly in Eagle Mountain's subdivisions developed between roughly 2005 and 2018: builders frequently installed black, 6-mil poly sheeting as a vapor barrier — technically meeting minimum code at the time — but left the edges loose and untaped against the foundation walls. Over years of foot traffic during HVAC or plumbing service visits, that sheeting gets torn, bunched, and displaced, leaving large sections of bare soil exposed. By the time a homeowner notices a musty smell upstairs, the wood subfloor above the exposed areas has often been absorbing vapor for multiple seasons. On these jobs we always pull the old sheeting entirely rather than layering over it, because trapping existing moisture between barriers creates worse conditions than starting fresh.
