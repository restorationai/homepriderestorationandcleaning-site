Park City's mountain climate creates a mold problem that most Utah homeowners don't see coming: heavy snowpack melts slowly into foundation walls and crawl spaces, and the town's dramatic freeze-thaw cycles force moisture into places it has no business being. When that moisture lingers inside a ski chalet in Deer Valley or a century-old Victorian near Old Town, mold colonies can establish themselves in as little as 48 to 72 hours — often behind walls that look perfectly dry from the outside. Home Pride Restoration and Cleaning has been handling mold remediation across the Wasatch Back since 1997, and we know how differently this mountain environment behaves compared to the valley floor.

## Why Park City Properties See Elevated Mold Risk

Elevation matters. Park City sits above 6,900 feet, which means longer winters, more total snowfall, and a short but intense spring thaw that saturates soil faster than drainage systems can handle. Homes in the Old Town Historic District — many built in the late 1800s during the silver-mining boom — were constructed with rubble-stone foundations and minimal vapor barriers. Those foundations wick groundwater upward through capillary action all spring and into early summer. Newer construction in the Deer Valley and Canyons Village resort corridors isn't immune either: high-end ski properties with heated driveways and complex HVAC systems often trap humid air in mechanical rooms, wine cellars, and ski lockers where ventilation is an afterthought.

The short shoulder seasons also mean properties sit vacant for weeks at a time with HVAC systems in setback mode. A slow drip from a supply line or a failed window seal can go unnoticed long enough for mold to colonize insulation, subfloor sheathing, and wall cavities — especially in homes with the spray-foam insulation common in post-2005 resort construction, where moisture has nowhere to migrate and no way to dry naturally.

## Our Mold Remediation Process in Park City

Every job starts with a thorough visual inspection and moisture mapping using thermal imaging cameras and calibrated moisture meters — not a quick walk-through. In Park City's older homes, we pay particular attention to rubble foundations, original plaster-and-lath walls, and any area within four feet of an exterior wall or below-grade surface. Once we understand the full extent of the problem, we establish negative-air containment using 6-mil poly barriers and HEPA-filtered air scrubbers to prevent cross-contamination to the rest of the structure.

Contaminated materials — drywall, insulation, framing when necessary — are removed, double-bagged, and disposed of per Utah Division of Waste Management guidelines. Remaining structural surfaces are treated with EPA-registered antimicrobial agents and dried to manufacturer-specified moisture content before any reconstruction begins. We are IICRC Certified, and our process follows the IICRC S520 Standard for Professional Mold Remediation. For properties where an HOA or a lender requires documentation, we can coordinate post-remediation clearance testing with an independent industrial hygienist before the job is closed out.

## Local Note: What We've Learned Working in Park City

One pattern we've seen repeatedly in Old Town and the streets just off Park Avenue: homes built before 1920 often have original horsehair-plaster walls over wood lath, and that plaster absorbs and holds moisture very differently from modern drywall. It dries significantly more slowly — sometimes taking two to three times as long as standard 5/8-inch gypsum board — and it can test as "dry" on a surface pin meter while still harboring elevated moisture content deeper in the substrate. We use both pin and pinless meters in combination on older Park City homes to avoid declaring a structure dry before it actually is. Rushing that step is how mold comes back six months later.
