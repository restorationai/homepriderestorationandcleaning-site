## Why American Fork Crawl Spaces Struggle More Than You'd Expect

The housing stock along the older residential streets near the American Fork canyon corridor includes a significant number of homes built in the 1970s and 1980s, when vapor barriers were either thin polyethylene sheeting or skipped entirely. Those barriers — where they exist — are typically 6-mil plastic that has since torn, shifted, or been punctured by pest activity. Meanwhile, Utah County's clay-heavy soil retains water longer than sandy soil does, so after a hard rain or a spring thaw, the ground beneath a crawl space can stay saturated for weeks. That sustained moisture wicks upward into floor joists, insulation batts, and subfloor sheathing.

The area also sits in FEMA flood zone designations that affect parts of the city closest to the American Fork River corridor. Homes in those zones tend to see higher average crawl space humidity readings year-round — often above 70% relative humidity without any encapsulation — which is the threshold at which mold species begin colonizing wood surfaces within 24 to 48 hours.

## Our Crawl Space Encapsulation Process in American Fork

Every encapsulation job starts with a full crawl space inspection: we measure humidity at multiple points, check for standing water or efflorescence on the foundation walls, probe joists for soft spots, and photograph existing vapor barrier condition. In American Fork homes with pier-and-beam foundations — common in neighborhoods developed before 1990 — we pay particular attention to the piers themselves, since they can wick ground moisture directly into the structural framing.

From there, the process runs in a defined sequence:

1. **Remove and dispose of old vapor barrier material** — torn poly sheeting traps debris and mold spores underneath; it comes out before anything new goes in.
2. **Address any standing water or active seepage** — we identify the source (often a cracked stem wall or a grading issue against the foundation) and correct it before sealing.
3. **Install 20-mil reinforced polyethylene liner** across the crawl space floor and up the foundation walls, sealed at all seams with moisture-resistant tape and mechanically fastened at the perimeter.
4. **Seal vents and penetrations** — in American Fork's climate, vented crawl spaces allow humid outside air to condense on cooler surfaces inside; closing vents is part of converting to a conditioned or semi-conditioned space.
5. **Install a crawl space dehumidifier** sized to the square footage, with a condensate drain routed to daylight or a sump.
6. **Final humidity reading and documentation** — we leave you with before-and-after readings so you have a baseline for future reference.

The entire process for a typical American Fork single-family home — roughly 1,000 to 1,400 square feet of crawl space — runs two to three days depending on access and existing conditions.
